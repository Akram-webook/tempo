# The KPI Cascade — every level accountable, top to bottom

A strategy to present to the Director: how the department's goal flows down so the
director, the senior manager, each manager, and every specialist all have KPIs — and
how each level is measured on the *right* thing. Researched and sourced. The data in
Webook Pass is mocked.

## 1. The big idea: a golden thread, not a rigid chain

The Director sets a few clear **department objectives** (e.g., "deliver every Tier-1
event on-time, on-budget, with zero burnout-driven resignations"). Each level then writes
its *own* KPIs that serve that intent — and we draw the links so anyone can trace their
KPI up to the department goal. That traceability is the **"line of sight" / golden thread**.

Important nuance (so it's robust, not bureaucratic): don't force a mechanical chain where
each person's number is literally their boss's number. Best practice is **align to the
intent, then connect** — each level owns level-appropriate KPIs, and we visualize the
linkage. Rigid metric-for-metric cascades are brittle and kill ownership.

## 2. What each level is measured ON (the core of the strategy)

The unit of accountability **changes** as you go up: an IC is measured on their own work;
a manager on their *team*; a senior on their *managers*; the director on the *department*.

| Level | Measured on | Example KPIs |
|---|---|---|
| **Specialist** (IC) | own execution | on-time task completion · quality/rework rate · vendor/issue resolution time · CSAT on owned deliverables · 1 skill-growth goal |
| **Manager** (Akram) | the **team** + people leadership | team on-time/on-budget/to-spec delivery · team quality/incident rate · **Team Health %** · talent development (skill growth, promotion-readiness) · **regretted attrition** · fair load distribution |
| **Senior Manager** (Motaa) | the **managers** + portfolio | portfolio delivery across teams · capacity vs event-pipeline demand · manager effectiveness/bench · # managers coached to readiness · department engagement & attrition (rolled up) |
| **Director** (Ahmed) | the **department** + org health | department events delivered / cost / growth · dept-wide quality & satisfaction index · org health (engagement, regretted attrition, internal-promotion rate, succession depth) · capacity vs demand · budget vs plan |

**The golden thread in one line:** the specialist's *on-time task* rolls up into the
manager's *team on-time delivery*, into the senior's *portfolio delivery*, into the
director's *events delivered*. Same thread — but each level **also** owns people/strategy
KPIs the level below doesn't, which is what prevents double-counting.

## 3. Why a manager is measured on the TEAM, not on his own tasks

This is the point that makes the Director nod. A great individual performer is not
automatically a great manager — the job changed. Research: managers account for ~70% of
the variance in team engagement (Gallup), and the top behavior of effective managers is
*coaching and developing people* (Google Project Oxygen), not personal output. So a
manager's scorecard = **team outcomes + leadership behaviors**, roughly 50–60% / 40–50%.

**Fairness guardrail (the controllability principle):** you can't hold a manager 100%
accountable for results he doesn't fully control, or it feels unfair and he games it. The
fix: **blend** the (partly uncontrollable) team-outcome KPIs with leadership-behavior KPIs
he *does* control — coaching cadence, development plans created, fairness of load,
onboarding. The higher the level, the more outcome-weighted (more control over strategy
and resources); front-line managers lean a bit more on behaviors and contribution.

## 4. The clever part: Team Health is the manager's KPI (anti-squeeze)

Make **Team Health %** (the share of the team in the healthy capacity band) and **regretted
attrition** scored manager KPIs. This structurally rewards *balance* over *squeezing*:
a manager who overloads people to hit a delivery number scores **worse** on Team Health and
attrition — so the incentive flips toward sustainable delivery. Webook Pass already computes
Team Health for any manager's team (open it as that manager via "View as"), so this KPI is
**measurable today**, not theoretical. Pair every delivery KPI with this health guardrail so
no one can look good on output while the team degrades.

## 5. No double-counting (keeps it fair)

Lower levels **own the delivery metric**; higher levels own the **aggregate + enabling**
metrics. The specialist is scored on *his* on-time %, the manager on the *team's* on-time %
+ development + health, the senior on *portfolio* + manager development — the same delivery
number isn't triple-scored as a top-line KPI at every level.

## 6. How to roll it out (and what to tell the Director)

**The pitch (30 seconds):**
> "I want every level to have 3 KPIs that all trace up to your department goal. Specialists
> are measured on their own delivery; managers on their team's delivery *plus team health and
> people growth* — so managers are rewarded for building sustainable teams, not for burning
> people out; seniors on their managers and the portfolio; and you see the whole department
> roll up in one view. Webook Pass already gives us the Team-Health number to make the manager
> KPI real. We pilot it on my team for a month, then scale it."

**Why he'll like it:** it gives him a single line of sight from his goal down to every
person; it makes managers accountable for *retention and balance* (directly attacking the 3
resignations); it's fair (controllability built in); and it's measurable now, not a theory.

**Rollout:** (1) Director approves 2–3 department objectives. (2) Each level drafts 3 KPIs
honoring that intent, co-created with their people. (3) Draw the linkage map (the golden
thread). (4) Weekly 15-min check-ins + monthly review, loop closed in writing. (5) Pilot on
one team (the silent POC), bring evidence, then scale. Keep individual scores private;
correct in private (Saudi/GCC face norms — see EVALUATION-and-KPIs.md).
