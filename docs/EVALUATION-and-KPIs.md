# Evaluating & Tracking the Team — a KPI playbook (global + Saudi)

How to evaluate your people fairly, what to hold them accountable on, how to handle
"this one needs follow-up / only works when directed," and how to roll KPIs out to a
Saudi/GCC team. Researched and sourced; built to present to the team. All data in the
app is mocked.

## 1. On what basis do you evaluate? Results **and** behaviors — never one number

Evaluate every person on **two axes**, not a single score:
1. **Results / impact** — what they actually delivered (events run, quality, budget).
2. **Behaviors / competencies** — *how* they delivered (ownership, teamwork, skill growth).

Why: a single number hides trade-offs and gets gamed; a target "can be hit badly"
(on-time by cutting corners). The balanced-scorecard principle (Kaplan & Norton) is
that no one measure can focus attention on all the critical areas. The biggest fairness
trap is the **activity-vs-impact** problem: hours, messages, and reports are *visible*
but measure presence, not progress. **Reward impact; use activity only as an early
signal.** (This is also why capacity/load in Webook Pass is never the performance score.)

## 2. KPIs vs OKRs vs leading/lagging (the part to teach the team)

- **KPI** = a health gauge you always watch (on-time %, CSAT). **OKR** = a quarterly
  improvement goal with soul. Use KPIs to monitor business-as-usual; spin up an OKR when
  a KPI goes unhealthy.
- **Leading vs lagging** — the most useful idea for your team:
  - **Lagging** = the result, already happened, low control (on-time delivery, incident count, CSAT).
  - **Leading** = the lever you can pull this week (checklist completion, milestone adherence, vendor response time).
  - **Pair every lagging outcome with a leading driver you control.** That's how a KPI
    becomes actionable instead of a verdict.

## 3. KPI menu for events-operations (pick 3–5 per person)

| KPI | Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| On-time delivery rate (milestones hit by promised date) | Lagging | the headline reliability metric |
| Milestone / prep-checklist completion | **Leading** | the controllable driver of on-time |
| Budget / cost variance vs plan | Lagging | financial discipline |
| Issue / vendor response & resolution time | **Leading** | speed under pressure |
| Defect / rework / incident count | Lagging | quality & safety |
| Inspection / process-deviation findings | **Leading** | predicts the next event's incidents |
| Attendee / stakeholder satisfaction (CSAT / post-event) | Lagging | did it land |
| Vendor SLA compliance | **Leading** | supplier reliability |

Keep it to **max ~3 KPIs per person** (focus beats coverage), each **SMART** (specific,
measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound) with one owner, a target, and a review
cadence. Benchmarks from vendor blogs are directional — set your own baseline first.

## 4. Accountability without micromanagement

- **One Accountable owner per event/task (RACI).** Many can be Responsible; exactly one
  signs off. "Everyone owns it → no one does" is the failure mode.
- **Weekly cadence of accountability** (≤20-min): each person reports (1) did I hit last
  week's commitments, (2) effect on the scoreboard, (3) my commitments for next week —
  committing to *peers*, not just you. Weekly feedback is one of the strongest engagement
  drivers (Gallup).
- **Close the loop:** every 1:1 ends with action / owner / deadline, a written recap, and
  review at the next 1:1. Commitments you don't track die.
- Aim for **high accountability + high psychological safety** (Edmondson's "learning
  zone"). Surveillance backfires — it kills the ownership that actually drives performance.

## 5. "Needs follow-up" vs "self-starter" — a stage, not a label (now in the app)

Your exact ask — *"this person needs chasing," "that one only works when directed"* —
is best handled with **Situational Leadership (Hersey-Blanchard / Blanchard SLII)**. It
turns a complaint into a management plan, and it's now a field on each profile
("Working style & how to manage"):

| Stage | Looks like | Your move |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-directed** | owns it | **Delegate** — give ownership, step back |
| **Capable but cautious** | can do it, hesitates | **Support** — encourage, hand over the decision |
| **Developing** | growing, motivation dips | **Coach** — direction + lots of feedback |
| **Needs direction & follow-up** | only moves when told | **Direct** — clear steps, frequent check-ins |

Two rules that keep it fair: **(1) it's task-specific** — the same person is self-directed
on a familiar event and "needs direction" on a brand-new venue; **(2) it's temporary** —
your job is to move them toward self-direction, not to brand them. The app shows a
"Needs follow-up" chip only as the current state, with that caveat written on-screen.
(Note: Situational Leadership is a great shared language but has weak empirical proof —
use it as a heuristic, not gospel. Pair it with **Initiative** as a rated competency.)

## 6. Rolling it out to a Saudi / GCC team

- **Lead it personally, explain the why.** High power distance means the manager's (and a
  senior sponsor's) visible ownership gives the system legitimacy — but tie it to a shared
  goal (event quality, guest experience, Vision 2030 growth), since change lands when it's
  seen as good for the group. Saudis already know KPI language from the national context
  (Adaa / Vision 2030) — use that familiarity.
- **Frame as development & clarity, not surveillance.** "Here's how to succeed here" beats
  "here's how we catch failure." Say explicitly it's for coaching, recognition, and growth.
- **Correct in private, always.** The most consistent regional finding: never criticize
  someone publicly — it damages face and the relationship. Praise in public, correct 1:1,
  be specific about the behavior (not the person), and confirm the expectation clearly.
- **Co-create the KPIs** and use self-assessment before reviews — raises ownership, lowers
  defensiveness. Keep individual scores private; keep the *rules* transparent.
- **Mixed-nationality teams:** apply identical criteria, vary delivery style per person —
  individual variation outweighs any national generalization. Fairness perception is the
  make-or-break variable, especially where pay differs by nationality.
- **Evolution not revolution:** pilot a few KPIs, expect it to take longer to land, build
  rapport first. (Webook Pass's 1-month silent POC fits this perfectly.)

## 7. My recommendation (what I'd actually do)

1. **Separate the two systems, share the backbone.** Keep *capacity/workload* (this tool)
   for "can I load them?" and *performance/KPIs* for "how did they do?" — never let load %
   become the grade. You've already built this separation.
2. **Pick 3 KPIs per person**, each with a leading lever, agreed *with* them. Start with:
   on-time delivery (+ milestone completion as the leading lever), quality/incident rate
   (+ inspection findings), and stakeholder satisfaction.
3. **Weekly 15-min check-in + monthly review**, close the loop in writing every time.
4. **Use the Working-style field** to set each person's management mode this quarter, and
   move the "needs follow-up" people up a stage with deliberate coaching.
5. **Don't bolt bonuses onto a single metric.** Use the results+behaviors view and the
   promotion-readiness signal as *inputs to your judgment*, reviewed in calibration — not
   an automatic formula. This protects fairness and keeps the data honest.
6. **Present it as "how to win here,"** correct privately, and run the silent POC first so
   you bring the Director evidence, not theory.
